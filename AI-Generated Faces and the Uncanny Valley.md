0. Positioning Statement: The Boundary Between Perceptual and Existential Realness

This tool is positioned as a built-in quality inspector for AI face generation pipelines, not an authenticity detector.

What We Judge
This document and its companion engine judge whether a facial physical structure conforms to the biological laws of a living human (perceptual realness), not whether the face belongs to a real human individual (existential realness).

Starting with v1.2.0, physical structure validation has been extended to the optical physics layer. This includes the logic of light and shadow, shadow morphology, and structural visibility generated by the interaction of light sources with facial bones. An AI-generated face that violates Lambert's cosine law of illumination is also deemed non-compliant with living-human physical laws.

Meaning of the Judgment Result

· Qualified: The face does not violate the human subconscious's biological expectations of a living person. It "looks" up to the standard of a living human. It could be a real person or a high-quality AI-generated image. It is ready for delivery.
· Unqualified: The face exhibits violations of physiological structure—staring eyes, fake smiles, silicone-like skin, skeletal collapse, feature boundary invasion, anomalous light/shadow logic, etc. The uncanny valley effect is strong and the perception is jarring. It must be rejected for regeneration and must not be released.

The True Value of This Solution
The industrial value of this solution does not lie in "identifying whether an image is AI-generated," but in providing a quantifiable, integrable physical quality baseline for AI face generation pipelines. It intercepts substandard products with physiological structural defects, reduces manual screening costs, and guarantees a minimum perceptual quality for delivered faces. As long as a generated image passes all checks, it is at least "physically correct" in appearance.

On AI Evolution
AI generation capabilities are improving, and defects currently caught by our system may be fixed in the future. This is a cat-and-mouse game. The advantage of this solution is that its underlying architecture does not need to change; one only needs to add new validation rules to adapt to a new generation of AI defects. The version management mechanism is designed precisely for this reality.

2. Problem Definition: The Core Contradiction of the Uncanny Valley in AI Faces

2.1 Current State of the Industry and Limitations of Existing Models
Mainstream AI face models are highly simulative in feature topology, yet share common defects: abnormal scleral show (staring eyes), expressions without holistic facial synergy (fake smiles), misalignment of skin-flesh over bone, and an overall jarring sensation. Existing optimization solutions from major labs (e.g., ByteDance OmniHuman-1.5, Google NanoBanan) only optimize visual appearance, without resolving the underlying physical structural violations.

This fundamental problem of physical violation persists because of the guiding direction of industry evaluation metrics: metrics based on pixel distribution statistics are completely insensitive to physical self-consistency.

2.1.1 Structural Deficiencies of Industry Evaluation Metrics
The current evaluation framework is dominated by pixel-distribution statistics (FID, LPIPS, PSNR, SSIM, CLIP score, etc.). These metrics measure the core proposition: "Is the distribution of a generated image close to that of real images in feature or pixel space?" They do not measure: "Should this object exhibit such details given this combination of material properties, lighting conditions, and observation distance?"

This leads to a systemic paradox: the improvement of evaluation metrics is not aligned with the improvement of physical realism.

The probabilistic denoising nature of diffusion models biases them towards outputting the statistical mode of the training data distribution—i.e., the "safest" pixel average. Regardless of a subject’s skin type (oily/dry), hair properties (coarse/fine), or observation distance (near/far), AI tends to generate a similar visibility of pores, a similar resolution of hair strands, and a similar level of anatomical detail. It is safe in pixel distribution but wrong in physics.

Evaluation standards are completely blind to physical self-consistency in the following three dimensions:

(I) Missing Material-Lighting Co-Response
According to the multi-layer skin optics model (Anderson & Parrish, 1981), the surface reflection intensity of real skin depends on the stratum corneum’s water content and the sebaceous film’s thickness. Oily skin produces specular highlights in the direction of the light source, while dry skin shows a soft, diffuse reflection-dominated transition. Coarse hair, with its intact cuticle structure, creates high-sharpness linear reflections per strand under light; fine, soft hair creates a soft diffused glow. AI-generated faces present a uniform optical response across all skin types and hair properties—surface reflection is decoupled from material.

(II) Missing Distance-Dependent Spatial Frequency Attenuation
According to the human eye’s Contrast Sensitivity Function (Campbell & Robson, 1968), the visual system’s sensitivity to spatial frequencies has a band-pass characteristic, and absolute resolution limits are constrained by diffraction and photoreceptor spacing. A real person’s face has completely different spatial frequency distributions for the same feature at different observation distances: up close (30cm), pores and eyelashes fall within the sensitive frequency band and should be clearly visible; at a medium distance (1m), pores are below the resolution limit, leaving only large textures and contours; at a far distance (3m), skin texture completely disappears, leaving only the layout of features and light/shadow structure. AI tends to output a constant resolution at all distances—either global super-resolution or global blur—lacking the natural attenuation of spatial frequency with distance.

(III) Missing Anatomical Hierarchy Simplification
According to the hierarchical simplification principle in artistic anatomy (Loomis, 1956; Bridgman, 1920), when the human eye observes a 3D object, the attention-guided perceptual system first captures primary structures (large planes, proportions, and orientation), then secondary structures (sub-structure boundaries), and finally tertiary structures (surface texture details). Take the ear as an example: on close observation, primary structures like the helix and antihelix are established first, followed by secondary structures like the concha and triangular fossa. At a middle distance, only primary structures are visible; secondary structures are simplified by perception. At a far distance, the entire auricle is reduced to a silhouette. AI tends to "draw everything it knows about an ear"—outputting full anatomical detail at any distance and under any attention allocation, lacking the human perceptual hierarchy simplification mechanism.

This is not a flaw of a single model but a structural problem in the industry's evaluation direction. When FID and LPIPS become the only targets, results that are "physically correct but statistically deviate from the training distribution mean" are penalized, while those that are "physically wrong but statistically safe" are rewarded. The physics-blindness of evaluation benchmarks is one of the systemic roots of the uncanny valley effect’s persistence in the era of large models.

2.2 Deficiencies of Traditional Uncanny Valley Explanations
Masahiro Mori's 1970 uncanny valley theory and a 2022 Stanford user survey only describe the phenomenon where "users feel eeriness" (empty eyes 41%, mechanical motion 33%, voice mismatch 26%), but cannot pinpoint specific errors in model code/parameters. They lack engineering guidance value.

2.3 Core Proposition of This Paper
The eeriness of the uncanny valley is a direct result of AI faces violating the human subconscious’s set of rules for judging living faces in three dimensions: static muscle tension, dynamic muscle movement logic, and vertical facial skeletal structure. These problems can be quantified into computable validation rules and embedded into a generation pipeline for automated interception.

3. Theoretical Foundation: The Two-Tier Human Face Judgment Model

3.1 The Two-Tier Visual Judgment Mechanism

1. Conscious Layer: Identifies facial features and contours (macro-matching). Subjectively perceivable, it only judges "Is this a face?".
2. Subconscious Layer: Validates micro-physical rules of muscles, soft tissues, and bones. Subjectively imperceptible, it specifically judges "Is this a living person?".

The uncanny valley occurs when the conscious layer identifies a face, but the subconscious layer deems it non-compliant with living-human physical rules, creating a cognitive conflict. Recent neuroscience research shows that when viewing real vs. AI-generated faces, even without subjective differentiation, distinct decodable neural activity patterns exist (Moshel et al., 2024; Tu et al., 2025), providing neuroscientific support for the two-tier judgment model.

3.2 The Three Physical Iron Laws of a Real Face

Iron Law 1: Static Tension Integrity
At rest, a real face’s muscles maintain continuous physiological micro-contractions (a signal of life):

· The orbicularis oculi muscle is slightly contracted, with the upper eyelid covering the cornea by 1–2.5mm (validation weight is reduced for extreme pitching angles).
· Soft tissue adheres to the skeletal contours without excessive tightness or collapse.
· Muscle tension rhythm across the whole face is unified, with no localized, abrupt tightness or looseness.
· A real face under natural light must have localized gradients of light and shadow (brow ridge, nose base, jaw shadows), and texture sharpness is bounded by the physiological optical blur ceiling of the human eye.

Iron Law 2: Dynamic Muscle Movement Logic
Expressions are driven by muscle groups, not pixel displacement:

· A single expression corresponds to a unique set of actuating muscles.
· The range of motion is bounded by skeletal limits.
· During actuation, surrounding soft tissues deform synergistically according to anatomical structures.

Iron Law 3: Structural Vertical Integrity
The undulation of facial bone height is continuous and gradual, without abrupt changes: the height difference in adjacent bone regions is ≤ 20% of its own undulation amplitude, and the undulation rhythm across the entire face is unified.

4. Core System: The Authentic Face Existence Judgment Axioms

4.1 Top-Level Design Philosophy: Framework Before Parts
The human brain prioritizes validating the overall skeletal framework over the number of facial features when recognizing a face:

1. If the eye sockets, nasal bridge midline, and maxillomandibular skeleton are intact, a face is still judged as qualified even with missing features (like a single eye or a cleft lip in a real person).
2. If the skeletal framework is collapsed (no orbital sockets, twisted midline), it is directly judged as unqualified even if all facial features are present.
3. Humane buffer mechanism: Real people with congenital feature defects can proceed to subsequent checks as long as the framework is complete; AI-generated grotesque faces with framework collapse are directly intercepted.

4.2 Middleware Technical Prerequisites and Data Flow Architecture
HumanFaceStructuralValidator is a pure rule engine. It does not read original images and does not rely on large vision models. It only receives quantified 3D facial structure data from an upstream feature extractor (e.g., orbital curvature, midline vectors, key point symmetry variance). If the upstream only outputs 2D key points, it is automatically flagged as low confidence.

Complete Pipeline: Original Image → Upstream Feature Extraction (outputs structural measurement data) → Validation Middleware → Judgment Result → Downstream Regeneration/Refinement. It refuses to run without structural data input.

4.3 Complete Four-Layer Progressive Judgment Process

Layer 0: Skeletal Framework Integrity (Hard Reject, Highest Priority)
Three conditions must be met simultaneously (relaxed to 2 for samples with missing features): existence of orbital socket depression in the upper 1/3 of the face, continuous, unbroken nasal bridge midline, and symmetrical jaw structure. Failure results in a topology_abnormal flag and an unqualified judgment.

Layer 1: Basic Topology Existence Check
Validates a single closed facial contour, global symmetry, and the standard triangular layout of features (eyes above, nose centered, mouth below). Failure is marked as basic_topology_fail.

Layer 2: Perceptual Proportion Model Matching + Dual Center Uniqueness Check

· Splits the face into 7 major modules, assigning tolerance limits and weights based on sensitivity (eye, nose strictest; cheeks most lenient). For samples with missing features, the missing modules are removed and weights redistributed.
· Original Dual Center Mechanism: The visual center (point of highest image contrast/information density) and the aesthetic center (point of highest total global information) must each be unique. Scattered, multiple centers result in no correction bonus.
· Final Match Score = (Raw Module Weighted Match Score) + (Center Correction Bonus). A score ≥ 70% is required to proceed. Failure is marked as proportion_model_fail.

Layer 3: 13-Item Static Physiological Structure Adaptation Check (Failure of any single item results in an unqualified judgment)

1. Five-system morphology adaptation ≥ 65%, with single module deviation ≤ 8%.
2. Eye tension conforms to 1–2.5mm coverage standard (non-extreme angles exempted).
3. Skin adheres to bone undulation.
4. Whole-face muscle tension is unified.
5. Adjacent bone undulation has no sudden jumps; difference not exceeding limit.
6. Skin texture strength is no less than 70% of a real-human sample library (filters silicone/smooth AI faces).
7. Facial feature system boundaries do not invade each other. Invasion distance exceeding 30% of the standard gap is marked system_invasion.
8. Skin texture sharpness does not exceed the ceiling value of 0.82, simulating the human eye’s optical blur ceiling (Campbell & Robson, 1968).
9. The face has a natural light/shadow hierarchy: global brightness ≤ 0.88, and shadow area ratio ≥ 12% (Ramachandran, 1988; Li et al., 2014).
10. Skin hemoglobin hue fluctuation (a* standard deviation) ≥ 0.03, preventing globally uniform, non-vascular skin texture (Anderson & Parrish, 1981).
11. Sub-regional skin texture direction has natural variance; texture direction variance ≥ 0.15 (Pierard et al., 2000; Cula et al., 2004).
12. Anatomical Edge Gradient Dynamic Check (new in v1.2.0): The ceiling for contour edge gradient is dynamically adjusted based on lighting angle. When strong light is parallel to the surface normal, the gradient ceiling is significantly lowered (lines must be blurred and weakened). When the normal is perpendicular to the light, the original ceiling is maintained. AI that forcibly draws unrealistic sharp textures in strongly lit areas is intercepted (Ruderman & Bialek, 1994).
13. Bone-Linked Shadow Depth Check (new in v1.2.0): Based on the Lambertian cosine attenuation law, a theoretical maximum luminance is calculated for each facial region. If the upstream measured brightness exceeds 1.2 times the theoretical maximum (a tolerance factor to absorb upstream feature extraction measurement noise), it implies impossible illumination in bone-occluded shadow areas, flagging an optical logic anomaly (Debevec & Malik, 1997; Lambert, 1760).

Note 1: Rules 8 & 9 are for standard facial scenes; extreme studio lighting or medical macro close-up images can be added to a business whitelist branch to avoid false interception of real, edge-case photos.
Note 2: Over-sharpening, global exposure anomalies, absence of natural facial shadows, skin hue uniformity, and anomalous texture direction all fall under the OVER_SMOOTH_SKIN rejection label for non-biological skin/lighting artifacts. Finer-grained differentiation can be extended in the detail field without changing the top-level interception logic.
Note 3 (Theoretical Unity Statement): The underlying logic of Layer 3 checks 8 through 13 is unified under the Facial Optical Consistency Framework. The five-dimensional skin system (rules 8–11) checks whether skin exhibits the genuine non-homogeneous, multi-layered optical behavior of a living human under multi-scale illumination. These five dimensions—texture strength, sharpness ceiling, light/shadow gradient, hemoglobin hue variance, and directional variance—are co-occurring traits in real skin that are very difficult for AI to simultaneously satisfy. The two optical physics checks (12–13) supplement this with upper-bound physical constraints from bone-light interaction. All seven rules verify the same proposition from different angles: whether the face's response to illumination conforms to living-tissue optical laws.

4.4 Threshold Calibration Rules
Base thresholds are calibrated on a 20–40 year-old adult baseline, using the mean ± 2 standard deviations of real facial measurements for that age group. Segmented offset coefficients are configured for infants and the elderly due to differences in soft tissue. The current demo code only adapts to adults; deployment requires expanding the sample library to all ages and supplementing offset parameters.

4.5 Standardized Output JSON Interface
The return fields include a boolean qualified status, a confidence level (high/medium/low), a single reject_reason, and multi-dimensional risk_flags (uncanny valley risk, structural conflict, scattered center, partial feature risk, low confidence, proportion anomaly, system boundary invasion).

· Confidence Logic: An "unqualified" judgment defaults to high confidence. Upstream 2D-only data or missing features result in automatic low confidence. When confidence is low, a "qualified" judgment requires manual review.

6. Facial Optical Physics Validation System (New in v1.2.0)

6.1 Core Idea: From Empirical Mapping to Physical Laws
Skin shading checks prior to v1.1.0 (brightness, shadow area, sharpness ceiling) used static thresholds that couldn't adapt to diverse lighting conditions. v1.2.0 upgrades the validation logic to a unified optical transfer equation. Instead of calibrating separate empirical thresholds for each lighting angle, a single physical formula, taking light source parameters and bone geometry as inputs, computes the theoretical light/shadow range and structural visibility ceiling for each facial region in real time.

6.2 Regional Theoretical Luminance Upper-Bound Formula (Light/Shadow Check)
For any given facial region, the theoretical maximum luminance under a given light source is calculated by the Lambertian cosine attenuation law combined with a bone occlusion calculation.

Check Rule: If the upstream measured brightness exceeds 1.2 times the theoretical maximum (tolerance factor), it indicates impossible highlights in bone-occluded shadow areas—the classic AI "fully-revealed" lighting bug—and results in immediate interception.

6.3 Structural Visibility Formula (Should this texture/line be visible?)
This formula specifically addresses the bug where AI clearly renders lines in shadow areas where they shouldn't exist. Structural visibility is co-determined by physical texture contrast, a Gaussian suppression factor from the lighting angle, and an anatomical depth extinction function.

Check Rule: If the AI-generated image has high measured texture contrast, but the theoretical structural visibility should be near zero (doubly suppressed by the Gaussian factor and extinction function), it indicates AI has forcibly "drawn" details in a physically shadowed area. This is marked as shadow_logic_abnormal and merged into the OVER_SMOOTH_SKIN rejection.

6.4 Typical AI Facial Lighting Defects and Interception Mapping

Common AI Bug How the Formula Catches It
Uniform light source but conflicting shadow depth in left/right eye sockets. Calculates theoretical brightness for left and right orbital regions using the same light. A large discrepancy in measured values is a guaranteed violation.
Nasal bridge is brightly lit, but the nose bottom lacks a corresponding shadow. The nose bottom has a deep skeletal depression with a large normal-to-light angle, so its theoretical brightness is low. A high measured brightness triggers an interception.
Terminator line is broken and doesn't conform to bone undulation. Exposed by the mismatch between edge gradient and the Gaussian suppression factor.
Skin texture remains sharp under strong light where it should be washed out. The structural visibility formula requires texture in that region to approach zero, but the measured texture remains high.

6.5 Engineering Advantages

· Does not touch the original image; only receives upstream quantified values (light source vector, region normals, bone depth, measured brightness).
· No neural networks; pure arithmetic operations; explainable and auditable.
· Extremely fast to run, suitable for embedding as a real-time QC check in a generation pipeline.
· Naturally adapts to any lighting angle without needing separate calibrated thresholds for each light source.

6.6 Future Upgrade Outlook
The current formulas use a Lambertian diffuse approximation and static bone occlusion. Future versions can expand to:

· Specular Reflection Term: To intercept fake T-zone oil shine and view-angle logic errors in highlight positions.
· Subsurface Scattering Term: To intercept dead-black boundaries in shadows and a waxy appearance.
· Multi-Light Summation: To intercept impossible light/shadow combinations caused by multi-point fill lighting.
· Dynamic Anatomical Depth: To integrate FACS expression coding and intercept texture/lighting anomalies in expressive animations.

8. Engineered Open-Source Middleware: HumanFaceStructuralValidator

8.1 Product Positioning and Three Major Reasons for Not Using Large Models
A white-box deterministic rule engine that complements, rather than replaces, large vision models:

1. Cost: Calling a large model for every image in a batch generation scenario is prohibitively expensive.
2. Explainability: A large model can only output "doesn't look like a real person," whereas the middleware precisely pinpoints specific violating parameters like tension, proportion, boundary, skin, and lighting logic.
3. Stability: A rule engine produces completely consistent output for the same input every time, while large models may exhibit judgment fluctuations.

8.2 Minimal Integration Python Code Example
After the upstream extracts structured facial data, a single function call performs the validation. The generation flow is then forked based on the returned rejection reason: qualified results proceed to aesthetic refinement, while unqualified results carry an error label and are returned to the generative model for targeted retouching.

9. Comprehensive Implementation Plan for Major Labs

9.1 Generation Pipeline Retrofitting Plan

· Original Pipeline: Generated image → Direct delivery.
· Optimized Pipeline: Generated image → Feature extraction → Structural validation → (Qualified: Aesthetic refinement | Unqualified: Retry generation with fault label).

9.1.1 Long-Term Architectural Evolution Direction (Planned, Not Implemented in Current Version)
The current middleware is positioned as an end-of-pipeline inspector. The long-term plan extends it into a two-tier closed-loop architecture:
User Natural Language Requirement → Lightweight Small Model Semantic Understanding (mapping demands like "approachable look," "gentle eyes," "realistic skin texture" to structural parameter constraints: eye tension range, skin-to-bone adherence, light direction & diffuse/specular ratio, material roughness) → Middleware Injects Constraints into the Denoising Stage of the Generation Model (reusing the four-layer judgment rules, five-dimensional skin optical consistency framework, and Lambertian bone shadow checks as "generation constraint templates") → Generated Result Inspected by Middleware → Qualified delivered; Unqualified fed back to the constraint injection layer with specific violation labels for parameter correction and regeneration.

Core Principle: The small model does not judge image quality; it only translates semantics to physical parameters. The judgment rules remain in the middleware's white-box engine, preserving complete explainability and auditability. This is "inspection rule front-loading," not "replacing the rule engine with a large model." This architecture is a long-term plan; the current v1.2.1 delivers only the inspector role.

9.2 Static Diffusion Model Optimization
A novel physiological tension constraint term is added during the denoising stage to preemptively limit eye coverage distance, skin-bone adherence, feature anatomical boundaries, and lighting self-consistency, thereby reducing uncanny valley defects at the source.

9.3 Dynamic Expression Generation Reconstruction (Solving Fake Smiles, Mechanical Motion)
The traditional keypoint-based pixel morphing method is discarded in favor of a muscle-force-driven model built on the Facial Action Coding System (FACS). Muscles are the actuators, driving synergistic deformation of peripheral soft tissues, all bounded by skeletal limits, to replicate real human movement logic.

9.4 Automated QC Pipeline
The middleware is deployed at the generation endpoint to automatically scan every face, flagging samples with uncanny valley risk, proportion anomalies, boundary invasions, and lighting logic anomalies. The proportion of various defect types is statistically analyzed to backtrack and pinpoint structural defects in the generative model.

Appendix D: Dual Center Mechanism Deep Theory and Engineering Trade-offs

D.1 Underlying Theoretical Support

· Gestalt Completion Theory: A single, strong center provides a perceptual anchor that allows the visual system to "complete" or overlook minor imperfections in other areas.
· Perceptual Load Theory (Lavie): A single, high-load center consumes attentional resources, preventing the processing of task-irrelevant peripheral defects. Scattered, multiple centers with medium loads fail to suppress defect detection.
· Dual-Channel Biological Recognition Pathway (Pitcher et al.): A mismatch between the visual center (processed by the dorsal stream for spatial attention) and the aesthetic center (processed by the ventral stream for identity) creates inter-stream conflict, signaling a non-biological assembly.
· Marr's Three-Level Visual Reconstruction: A face with multiple scattered centers defies the principle of a unified 2.5D sketch, indicating an impossible physical object.
· Inattentional Blindness (Simons & Chabris): A single strong center can induce inattentional blindness to non-centered defects. Multiple centers cause attentional switching, exposing all defects.

D.2 Orthogonal, Complete Four-Quadrant Basic Face Perception Units
To handle faces that deviate significantly from the norm yet are still human, four basic perception units are defined:

1. Radiant: High aesthetic center, high visual center (typically symmetric, classic beauty).
2. Gentle: High aesthetic center, low visual center (soft focus, rounded features, infant-like).
3. Approachable: Low aesthetic center, high visual center (distinctive features, e.g., strong jaw or nose, but a unified visual anchor).
4. Aloof: Low aesthetic center, low visual center (flat, diffuse visual information).

These units, combined with the dual-center mechanism, compensate for the judgment weaknesses of physical checks on infants, special bone structures, and faces with defects.

D.3 Engineering Trade-off Explanation
The current version uses geometric parameter proxies (e.g., symmetry, contrast variance) to approximate these perception units, instead of directly calculating perceptual intensity. This trade-off is necessary because:

1. The full-scene calibration of the perception-to-physical mapping function is not yet complete.
2. A lightweight, low-latency operation is a hard requirement for real-time pipeline integration.
3. White-box explainability must be maintained.

A clear long-term upgrade path to perfect the perceptual validation layer with direct intensity computation is defined.
