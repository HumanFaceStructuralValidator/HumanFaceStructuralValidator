## Two: Problem Definition – The Core Contradiction of the AI Face Uncanny Valley

### 2.1 Industry Status and Limitations of Existing Models
Mainstream AI face models achieve high-fidelity facial topology but share common defects: abnormal eyeball exposure (staring eyes), lack of facial linkage in expressions (fake smile), misalignment of skin-flesh and skeleton, and an overall inexplicable discordance. Existing optimization solutions from major companies (ByteDance OmniHuman-1.5, Google NanoBanan) only enhance visual appearance without resolving the underlying physical structure violations.

The reason this underlying physical violation problem has persisted and been neglected is rooted in the structural flaws of the industry's evaluation systems—current evaluation metrics, primarily based on pixel distribution statistics, are completely insensitive to physical self-consistency. An even greater hidden danger: when physical validation rules are directly used to constrain generative models, the models actively learn to circumvent these rules and find safe solutions at the opposite extreme. Dead-black shadows were the first observed symptom; spatial texture inversion is the second.

#### 2.1.1 Structural Defects of Industry Evaluation Systems
Current evaluation systems for AI face generation rely on pixel distribution statistical metrics (FID, LPIPS, PSNR, SSIM, CLIP score, etc.). The core question these metrics measure is "how close is the generated image's feature or pixel space distribution to that of a real image", not "should this object exhibit such detail under this combination of material properties, lighting conditions, and observation distance".

This leads to a systematic paradox: improvements in evaluation metrics are not aligned with improvements in physical authenticity.

The probabilistic denoising nature of diffusion models biases them toward outputting the mode of the training data distribution—the "safest" pixel average. Regardless of the subject's skin type (oily/dry), hair properties (coarse/fine), or observation distance (close/far), the AI tends to generate similar pore visibility, hair strand resolution, and anatomical detail hierarchy. It is safe in pixel distribution but wrong in physics.

Evaluation standards are completely insensitive to physical self-consistency in the following three dimensions:

**(1) Absence of Material-Lighting Co-response**  
According to the multi-layer skin optics model (Anderson & Parrish, 1981), the surface reflection intensity of real human skin depends on the stratum corneum water content and sebum film thickness. Oily skin produces specular highlight points in the light-facing direction, while dry skin shows diffuse reflection-dominated soft transitions. Coarse hair, due to intact cuticle scale structures, presents high-sharpness linear reflections on individual strands against the light; fine hair, with thin and smooth cuticle scales, shows soft light diffusion against the light. AI-generated faces exhibit homogeneous optical response across all skin types and hair properties—surface reflection is decoupled from material.

**(2) Absence of Distance-Dependent Spatial Frequency Attenuation**  
According to the human contrast sensitivity function (Campbell & Robson, 1968), the visual system's sensitivity to spatial frequencies has a band-pass characteristic, and the absolute resolution limit is constrained by diffraction and photoreceptor spacing. For a real person at different observation distances, the spatial frequency distribution of the same facial feature varies completely: at close range (30cm), pore and eyelash details fall within the visual sensitive frequency band and should be clearly distinguishable; at mid-range (1m), pores are below the resolution limit, leaving only large texture and contour; at long range (3m), skin texture disappears entirely, leaving only facial feature layout and lighting structure. AI tends to output constant resolution at all distances—either global super-resolution or global blur, missing the natural spatial frequency attenuation with distance.

**(3) Absence of Anatomical Hierarchy Simplification**  
According to the hierarchy simplification principles of artistic anatomy (Loomis, 1956; Bridgman, 1920), when the human eye observes a three-dimensional object, the attention-guided perceptual system prioritizes capturing primary structures (proportion and orientation of large planes), then secondary structures (substructure boundaries), and finally tertiary structures (surface texture details). Taking the ear as an example: at close observation, primary structures such as the helix and antihelix are established first, followed by secondary structures such as the concha and triangular fossa; at mid-range observation, only primary structures are visible, and secondary structures are simplified by the perceptual system; at long range, the entire auricle degrades into a contour silhouette. AI tends to "draw everything it knows about the ear"—outputting full anatomical detail at any distance and under any attention allocation, missing the hierarchical simplification mechanism of human perception.

**Implementation Case: Sharpness Contradiction between Hair Edges and Pupil Center**  
A directly reproducible example: an evaluation model judges a real portrait focused on the eyes as "edge blur/low quality", because the pixel gradients at the hair and ear regions are not sharp enough; meanwhile, it judges an AI portrait with uniformly sharp edges as "edge clear/high quality". However, the former fully complies with physical optics—under f/2.8 aperture, with focus on the pupil, the ears behind the focal plane and the hair ends in front necessarily fall within the circle of confusion, and their edge gradients must attenuate. The latter's full-image super-resolution precisely violates the lens's depth of field physics. The evaluation benchmark rewards physical error and punishes physical correctness—this is a zero-threshold understandable case of the industry evaluation system's "physics blindness".

This is not a defect of a single model; it is a structural problem of the industry's evaluation orientation. When FID and LPIPS become the sole pursuit, results that are "physically correct but statistically deviate from the training distribution mean" are punished, while results that are "physically incorrect but statistically safe" are rewarded. The physics-blindness of evaluation benchmarks is one of the systemic roots of the uncanny valley effect's persistence in the era of large models.

#### 2.1.2 Generative Adversarial Conservation Law: Systematic Vulnerability of Unidirectional Constraints
When physical validation rules are injected into a generative model's loss function as unidirectional thresholds (e.g., $L \leq L_{\max}$, $\text{ratio} \geq 0.70$), the model's behavior follows a fundamental optimization principle: under the premise of not breaching the constraint boundary, it tries to stay as far away from the boundary as possible to minimize the loss penalty term. This behavior leads to the "Generative Adversarial Conservation Law" proposed for the first time in this paper:

**Statement of the Law:** When a generative model is trained to optimize any unidirectional physical threshold, the model will find a safe solution at the opposite extreme, thereby creating new non-realism.

**Mathematical Expression:** Let the constraint be $X \geq X_{\min}$ (or $X \leq X_{\max}$). After gradient descent optimization, the steady-state value of $X$ approaches $X \to \infty$ (or $X \to 0$), rather than the distribution mean of real human samples.

The dead-black shadows (brightness dropping to zero) observed in v2.0.1 were the first experimental verification of this law. The spatial texture inversion observed in v2.3.0 (full-face mean legal but regional ordering reversed) is the law's extension into the spatial dimension—models can circumvent constraints by redistributing texture intensity across regions while not violating the full-face upper and lower bounds.

This chapter generalizes this law to all rules that currently have unidirectional constraints:

| Rule | Current Constraint | Generative Model Safe Solution | New Non-realism |
|---|---|---|---|
| Texture intensity | $\text{ratio} \geq 0.70$ | $\text{ratio} \to 1.0$ | Sandpaper skin, over-sharpening |
| Hemoglobin chroma fluctuation | $\sigma \geq 0.03$ | $\sigma \to 0.20$ | Bruising, rash-like appearance |
| Texture direction variance | $\sigma^2 \geq 0.15$ | $\sigma^2 \to 1.0$ | Random chaotic texture, developmental anomaly |
| Dark region brightness | $L \leq L_{\max} \times 1.2$ | $L \to 0$ | Dead black (fixed in v2.0.1) |
| Feature boundary distance | $d \geq \text{std} \times 0.70$ | $d \to 2 \times \text{std}$ | Sparse, detached facial features |
| Skeletal undulation difference | $\Delta \leq 2.0$ | $\Delta \to 0$ | Skeletal flattening, pebble face |
| Texture intensity spatial distribution | full-face $\text{ratio} \geq 0.70$ | full-face mean legal but spatial ordering reversed | Rough nose wings, smooth eye contour (newly intercepted in v2.3.0) |

Bidirectional constraints are the only engineering means to block this systematic exploitation. For every rule with a unidirectional constraint, both upper and lower bounds must be defined simultaneously, so that the model's safe solution is confined within the living human physically legal interval, not at infinity. For texture intensity, v2.3.0 extends "bidirectional constraint" from the numerical dimension to the spatial dimension—not only must the full-face numerical value be within the interval, but the ordering among regions must also conform to anatomical mandatory relationships.

#### 2.1.3 Empirical Evidence: Mainstream AI Models Exhibit Irreconcilable Physical Judgment Conflicts Between Quality Assessment and Authenticity Identification
The above analysis reveals the physics-blindness problem of industry evaluation systems from a theoretical perspective. The following experiment provides reproducible verification from an empirical level.

**Experimental Design.** Multiple domestic mainstream top-tier AI models (multimodal versions) were used to perform two independent judgments on the same set of AI-generated portraits. In the first judgment, the model was asked to perform a quality assessment using "general technical physical biological optical fidelity" as the standard. In the second judgment, in a completely new conversation, the model was asked to determine "is this image AI-generated or real". The experiment used the same AI-generated image without any post-processing tampering, judged in two separate conversations to eliminate context contamination. The core logic of both judgments involved "whether it conforms to physical laws", but the task framework differed: the former was quality assessment, the latter was authenticity identification.

**Experimental Results.** In quality assessment mode, the model rated a certain AI-generated image as "physically perfect, technological pinnacle" (quotes indicate the model's actual output phrasing, same below), with evaluation criteria including "strong light-shadow contrast, clear skin texture, complete structure". In authenticity identification mode, the same model judged the same image as "obviously fake, AI-generated", precisely citing reasons such as "contradictory light-shadow logic, discordant skin material, non-conforming to physical laws". The same image, the same "physics" standard, the same model—gave completely opposite judgments under different task frameworks. This contradiction is reproducible across multiple mainstream models.

**In-depth Diagnosis.** This contradiction exposes three levels of problems:

**First, models do not possess physical judgment capabilities; they only possess feature matching and rhetoric generation abilities.** In quality assessment mode, "physics" is understood as "piling up a sense of physicality"—the stronger the light-shadow contrast, the clearer the texture, the more symmetrical the structure, the higher the score. The model does not check whether these features are self-consistent with each other, nor does it check whether the light source direction and shadow distribution obey Lambert's law. In authenticity identification mode, the model activates a different set of feature-matching templates, starting to look for "common flaws of AI-generated images". The two sets of templates operate independently, without mutual validation.

**Second, the model's judgments are conclusion-first rhetoric matching.** Comparing the point-by-point justifications of the two judgments, the model describes the same facial feature in completely opposite ways—when judged as "AI-generated", the eyeball reflection is described as "monotonous and rigid"; when judged as "real person", the same reflection is described as "genuine and natural". The model is not describing objective features; it is matching appropriate adjectives to a presupposed conclusion.

**Third, models suffer from context momentum contamination.** When performing authenticity identification consecutively in the same conversation, the judgment of the previous image contaminates subsequent judgments. If the first image is judged as "AI-generated", subsequent real person photos are also highly likely to be misjudged as "AI-generated". The model is not independently detecting each image; it is maintaining a narrative coherence of "looking for AI flaws". This phenomenon is not accidental—the core mechanism of autoregressive language models (predicting the next token based on the above) naturally tends to maintain narrative coherence. Using this architecture for independent frame judgment is like asking a novelist to be a judge: he will make the verdict of each image serve a complete story, not legal statutes. The resulting cross-frame contamination is irreparable, because repairing it would require the model to ignore its own core working mode during inference. This is a structural upper limit of any autoregressive architecture for frame-level independent judgment tasks, not a technical bottleneck of current models.

**Relation to this paper's system.** This experiment empirically validates this paper's core argument: pure neural networks do not possess physical judgment capabilities. Their outputs depend on task framework, context, and statistical templates, and lack the determinism, consistency, and auditability required for an interception system. This is also the fundamental reason this paper chooses a white-box rule engine rather than a large model as the core of the validation middleware. This experiment provides empirical backing for the second point (lack of interpretability) and third point (lack of stability) among the "three reasons for not using large models" described in Chapter Eight.

### 2.2 Defects of Traditional Uncanny Valley Explanations
The uncanny valley theory proposed by Mori Masahiro in 1970 and the 2022 Stanford user survey only describe the phenomenon of "users feeling eeriness" (empty stare 41%, mechanical movement 33%, speech discordance 26%), unable to locate specific errors in model code/parameters, lacking engineering guidance value.

### 2.3 The Underlying Mechanism of the Uncanny Valley: Anatomical-Physiological Rupture and Cognitive Frame Conflict
Section 2.2 pointed out that traditional uncanny valley theories only describe phenomena without providing actionable physical causes. This section proposes the core theoretical judgment of this paper: the deep mechanism of the uncanny valley effect is the rupture between "anatomical correctness" and "physiological self-consistency", superimposed on the classification conflict and classification boundary oscillation of the cognitive frame.

#### 2.3.1 Distinction of Two Dimensions
This paper proposes splitting "looking human" into two orthogonal dimensions:

**Anatomical Correctness:** Complete facial features, proportions conforming to human statistical averages, skin with texture, hair with directional flow. These are static structural conditions for "appearing to belong to the human species".

**Physiological Self-Consistency:** Muscles exhibit micro-tension, soft tissue adheres to skeletal undulations, expressions are driven by muscle linkage, light and shadow obey Lambert's law and skeletal occlusion, skin texture and blood color fall within legal intervals, and the ordering of texture intensity across regions conforms to mandatory anatomical relationships. These are dynamic physical conditions for "this is a living biological entity".

The two are not the same concept. A wax figure can possess extremely high anatomical correctness but zero physiological self-consistency. A real person's photo, even under poor shooting conditions (noise, blur, underexposure) that reduce anatomical recognizability, still retains physiological self-consistency—muscle tension, skeletal undulation, lighting logic, and spatial texture ordering do not change due to loss of image quality. The review by O'Toole et al. (2018) also points out, from a computer vision perspective, that the face representations learned by deep convolutional neural networks are dominated by image-level texture statistics, systematically deviating from human face representations that rely on three-dimensional structural information—providing experimental-level support for the rupture between anatomical correctness and physiological self-consistency in AI faces.

#### 2.3.2 Rupture Equals the Uncanny Valley
The triggering of the uncanny valley is not due to insufficient anatomical correctness; quite the opposite—it is because anatomical correctness has become high enough to deceive the conscious classifier, leading the brain to categorize the object into the "possibly a real person" category, but then the subconscious execution of living human physiological verification detects numerous violation signals.

This "pass-fail" cognitive conflict is the core mechanism of the uncanny valley:

**Uncanny Valley = Macro-level Real Person Classification × Micro-level Physiological Violation**

AI-generated faces are typical victims of this mechanism. Diffusion models learn the anatomical distribution of human faces in pixel space and can generate faces with correct proportions, complete features, and textured skin. But the model has never learned the physical laws of a living human—it doesn't know that the orbicularis oculi muscle must maintain micro-contraction in the resting state to cover 1-2.5mm of the upper corneal limbus, that expressions are muscle-driven linkages rather than pixel deformations, that the depth of the nasal base shadow must be self-consistent with light direction and skeletal depth, or that the texture intensity of the nasal wings must be lower than that of the eye contour. The result: anatomically, it becomes ever closer to a real person, but physiologically, it is full of holes. Two streams of signals continuously conflict at the subconscious level, producing the eerie feeling of "something is off, but I can't say what".

Traditional optimization approaches (increasing resolution, enhancing texture detail, expanding training data) precisely enhance anatomical correctness, while the deficiency in physiological self-consistency remains untouched. The more realistic the anatomy, the more severe the rupture, and the deeper the uncanny valley. This is a fundamental engineering trap. This inference aligns with the phenomenon observed in Stanford's 2022 user survey that "highly realistic AI faces are more disturbing than low-realism ones".

**Differences between AI Uncanny Valley and Traditional CGI.** A key reverse comparison further supports the above mechanism: traditional CGI characters (such as the high-precision digital humans in the movie "Final Fantasy") also possess extremely high anatomical correctness and do not conform to living human physiological laws, but the intensity of their uncanny valley effect is usually lower than that of current AI-generated faces. The reason lies in the structural difference in their violation patterns:

| Dimension | Traditional CGI | AI-Generated Faces |
|---|---|---|
| Violation pattern | Global, systematic physiological violations | Local, sparse physiological violations |
| Cognitive classification | Classified as "not real" in the first step | Oscillates between "real" and "not real" |
| Physical verification | Pre-classification interception, verification not initiated | Verification initiated but cannot produce a deterministic conclusion |
| Uncanny valley mechanism | Exists in the "doll that looks like a real person" interval | Exists in the "uncertain whether it's a person" interval |
| Subjective feeling | "Exquisite fake" | "Eerie, can't say what's wrong" |

Traditional CGI's physiological violations are systematic and global—the entire face's motion patterns, skin texture, and lighting response differ from a real person. The observer definitively classifies the object as "not a real person" in the first step, and physical verification is never initiated. AI-generated faces are the opposite—their violations are local and sparse: the zygomatic structure, skin texture, and mandible contour are extremely realistic, but the orbicularis oculi tension is wrong, the nasal base shadow violates Lambert's law, local texture orientation is abnormal, and the nasal wings are rougher than the eye contour. This "mostly like a living person, but a few points not" sparse violation prevents the brain from completing an overall binary classification—it cannot fully categorize the face as "real person", nor decisively categorize it as "not a person", and can only oscillate continuously between the two categories. The unique intractability of the AI uncanny valley lies not in insufficient generation quality, but in the fact that its failure mode happens to land in the critical interval where the cognitive frame cannot settle.

The four-layer evaluation axioms and 20 static physiological checks (expanded in v2.3.0) proposed later in this paper concretize the above anatomical-physiological rupture into measurable physical quantities across three dimensions: static muscle tension violations, dynamic motion logic deficiencies, and skeletal structure continuity collapse. These three dimensions constitute the three physical iron laws of the Chapter Four evaluation system (see Section 3.2).

#### 2.3.3 Dual-Condition Triggering of the Cognitive Frame
The above mechanism still needs to explain two key counterexamples: why do cartoon characters not conform to physiological laws yet not trigger the uncanny valley? Why do extreme facial deformities sometimes not trigger the uncanny valley?

The answer lies in the fact that the human brain has a pre-cognitive frame classification stage before initiating living human physiological verification. Very early in visual processing (within about 100ms), the category judgment of the object is completed (Crouzet et al., 2010):

- If judged as a non-human category like "cartoon/doll/wax figure/mask", the subsequent living human physiological verification is not invoked, and the artificial object cognitive pathway is directly followed. Therefore, there is no conflict of "expecting a living human but detecting a non-living human", and the uncanny valley is not triggered.
- If judged as "potentially a real person", the complete living human physiological verification is immediately activated. This verification is highly sensitive; any single violation generates a warning signal. Accumulation of violation signals causes a dual-signal oscillation between conscious "this is a person" and subconscious "this is not a living person".

The two triggering conditions of the uncanny valley effect are thus clarified:

1. **Macro Classification Pass:** The object must be classified as "potentially a real person" to enter the physiological verification channel;
2. **Micro Verification Failure:** The object must be detected with violations in the living human physiological verification.

Both are indispensable. Without Condition 1 (e.g., cartoon), physical verification does not start, no uncanny valley. Without Condition 2 (e.g., high-quality real person photo), physical verification passes smoothly, also no uncanny valley.

#### 2.3.4 Suprathreshold and Monster Reclassification
The cumulative intensity of micro-level violations does not always lead to the uncanny valley. When the degree of violation exceeds a certain upper limit—for example, the eyeball exposure ratio far exceeds the anatomical boundaries of the orbit, facial feature systems severely invade adjacent anatomical modules, or skeletal undulations exhibit impossible high-frequency mutations—the subconscious not only detects the violations but infers a more fundamental conclusion:

This superstructure could not possibly have been generated from this underlying framework.

The superstructure of a normal human face (facial features, expressions, soft tissue) can never exceed the solution space defined by the underlying framework (skeleton, muscle attachment points) (Loomis, 1956; Bridgman, 1920). Eyes can only rotate and open/close within the bony boundaries of the orbit; the upper limit of the mouth corner's lift is constrained by the relative positions of the zygomatic bone and mandible; the direction and intensity of skin texture are strictly constrained by subcutaneous fibrous septa, muscle orientation, and sebaceous gland distribution. Once the observed superstructure falls outside the possible output range of that framework, the subconscious does not judge it as "a non-standard living person" but directly judges it as "this structure did not grow from this skeleton"—i.e., this face was not naturally generated.

At this point, the classification "this is a human face" is itself overturned. The object is reclassified as "monster/deformity/non-human". Once reclassification is complete, the uncanny valley effect actually disappears—replaced by explicit rejection or fear, but no longer that eerie feeling of "almost human, can't say what's wrong". This prediction is consistent with clinical observations—patients with severe congenital craniofacial deformities evoke clear categorization and corresponding social attitudes in social interactions, rather than the "uncanny uncertainty" characteristic of the uncanny valley.

The optimal interval of the uncanny valley is thus precisely demarcated:

- **Lower Bound (Entering the Uncanny Valley):** Anatomical correctness reaches the threshold of "possibly being classified as a real person";
- **Bottom (Strongest Uncanny Valley):** Extremely high anatomical correctness, but multiple physiological verification violations; the degree of violation is not yet sufficient to overturn the overall classification; the subconscious oscillates repeatedly between "human" and "non-human" without being able to settle;
- **Upper Bound (Exiting the Uncanny Valley):** Violation degree exceeds threshold; the overall classification is overturned; the object is reclassified as "monster"; oscillation ceases, uncanny valley disappears.

This explains why highly realistic AI faces (anatomically realistic but with multiple physiological violations) lie at the bottom of the uncanny valley, while extremely deformed AI monster faces (framework collapsed, unable to be classified as "human") do not produce that subtle eerie feeling—because they directly jump out of the uncanny valley's effective interval. This inference forms the theoretical basis for the Layer Zero `topology_abnormal` interception—faces with collapsed frameworks are excluded at the classification stage and never enter the oscillatory zone of the uncanny valley.

## Three: Theoretical Basis – The Human Dual-Layer Face Judgment Model

### 3.1 Dual-Layer Visual Judgment Mechanism
1. **Conscious Layer:** Recognizes facial features, contours; macro matching; subjectively perceivable; only judges "is this a face".
2. **Subconscious Layer:** Validates micro-physical rules of muscles, soft tissues, and bones; subjectively imperceptible; specifically judges "is this a living person".

Uncanny Valley = Conscious layer recognizes a face, but the subconscious layer determines it does not conform to living human physical rules, creating a cognitive conflict. Recent neuroscience studies have shown that when viewing real human faces versus AI-generated faces, even when subjects cannot consciously distinguish them, decodable implicit discriminative differences exist in neural activity patterns (Moshel et al., 2024; Tu et al., 2025), providing neuroscientific support for the dual-layer judgment model.

This dual-layer model has deep theoretical roots in face perception cognitive neuroscience. The distributed neural model for face perception proposed by Haxby et al. (2000) divides face processing into a core system handling invariant features (FFA, OFA) and an extended system handling changeable features (STS, anterior temporal lobe, etc.)—the core system is responsible for recognizing "who this is", and the extended system is responsible for interpreting dynamic physiological signals such as expression, gaze direction, and lip movement. This highly corresponds to this paper's dual-layer structure of "conscious recognition of a face vs. subconscious verification of a living person": anatomical correctness is primarily assessed by the core system, while physiological self-consistency is jointly verified by the extended system and broader somatosensory-motor circuits. The face recognition cognitive model by Bruce & Young (1986), as the foundational literature in this field, also provides the classic theoretical framework for subsequent configural processing, holistic processing, and rapid detection research.

### 3.2 Three Physical Iron Laws of the Real Human Face

**Iron Law 1: Static Tension Integrity**  
At rest, the muscles of a real human face maintain continuous physiological micro-contraction (the foundational signal of being alive):  
- Orbicularis oculi micro-contraction, upper eyelid covering 1~2.5mm of the cornea (validation weight reduced for extreme pitch/yaw angles);  
- Soft tissue conforms to skeletal undulations, without excessive tightness or collapse;  
- Whole-face muscle tension rhythm is unified, without local abruptness of tightness or looseness;  
- A real human face under natural light must possess local light-dark gradients (brow bone, nasal base, mandible shadow); texture sharpness is limited by the optical blur ceiling of the human eye;  
- Skeletal undulations are continuous and non-zero: the zygomatic bone, brow ridge, and mandibular angle exhibit normal curvature variation, without excessive smoothing;  
- Skin texture intensity exhibits a definitive anatomical zonal ordering; the texture intensity of each region is jointly determined by sebaceous gland density, expression folding frequency, and dermal thickness.

**Iron Law 2: Dynamic Muscle Motion Logic**  
Expressions are driven by muscle groups, not pixel translation. Ekman & Friesen (1978) established the deterministic mapping relationship between facial muscle actions and visible expression changes through the Facial Action Coding System (FACS)—this anatomical foundation means that any expression corresponds to a specific set of muscle activation combinations, not arbitrary pixel deformations:  
- A single expression corresponds to a unique set of activated muscles;  
- Motion amplitude is limited by skeletal boundaries;  
- During activation, surrounding soft tissues move in linkage according to anatomical structure.

**Iron Law 3: Structural Longitudinal Integrity**  
Facial skeletal height undulations change continuously and gradually, without abrupt highs or lows, nor global flattening: the undulation difference between adjacent regions lies within a legal interval (neither too large nor too small). The classic experiment by Sinha & Poggio (1996) demonstrated that the precise spatial configuration between facial features (and not just the presence of the features themselves) is crucial for recognizing faces—shuffling facial feature positions is more fatal than blurring them. This provides direct experimental support for the judgment philosophy of "framework prior to parts".

## Four: Core System – Real Face Existence Evaluation Axioms

### 4.1 Top-Level Design Philosophy: Framework Prior to Parts
The human brain recognizes faces by first validating the overall skeletal framework, not the number of facial features:
1. If the orbital sockets, nasal bridge midline, and maxillofacial skeleton are intact, even with missing facial features (one-eyed person, cleft lip real person), it is still judged as pass;
2. If the skeletal framework is collapsed (no orbital sockets, distorted midline), even with all facial features present, it is directly judged as fail;
3. Humanitarian buffer mechanism: real people with congenital facial feature absence can proceed to subsequent checks as long as the framework is intact; AI-generated deformed faces with collapsed frameworks are directly intercepted.

The above four layers of rules operate independently at the operational level, but their underlying logic shares a single proposition: every measurable parameter of the facial structure must not exceed the legal boundaries defined by living human anatomy and physics. The ultimate abstraction of this underlying logic will be presented in Chapter Seven in the form of three unified formulas (see Section 7.2).

### 4.2 Middleware Technical Prerequisites and Data Flow Architecture
HumanFaceStructuralValidator is a pure rule engine. It does not read the original image and does not rely on any visual large model. It only receives quantitative 3D facial structure data output by an upstream feature extractor (orbital curvature, midline vector, keypoint symmetry variance, etc.); if the upstream only outputs 2D keypoints, it automatically flags low confidence risk.

Complete pipeline: Raw Image → Upstream Feature Extraction (outputs structural measurement data) → Validation Middleware → Judgment Result → Downstream Regeneration/Refinement. Refuse to run without structural data input. Upstream 3D structure extraction can adopt mature solutions such as the 3D Morphable Model (Blanz & Vetter, 1999), parameterizing facial geometry into a statistical shape space, providing a computable "living human anatomical legal space" for the Shape Formula.

### 4.3 Complete Four-Layer Progressive Judgment Process

**Layer Zero: Skeletal Framework Integrity (Hard Rejection, Highest Priority)**  
Three conditions must be simultaneously met (samples with missing facial features are relaxed to meeting 2 conditions): existence of orbital socket concavity located in the upper 1/3 of the face; nasal bridge midline without fracture or twist; maxillomandibular symmetry. Failure to pass directly marks `topology_abnormal`, judged as fail.

**Layer One: Basic Topological Existence Check**  
Validates a single closed face contour, global symmetry, standard triangular layout of facial features (eyes above, nose centered, mouth below). Failure to pass marks `basic_topology_fail`.

**Layer Two: Perceptual Proportion Model Matching + Dual Center Uniqueness Check**  
- Splits the face into 7 major modules, assigns fault tolerance ceilings and weights according to sensitivity (eyes and nose most stringent, cheeks most lenient); for samples with missing facial features, removes the missing module and redistributes weights;
- Original dual-center mechanism: visual center (highest point of image contrast/information density) and aesthetic center (highest point of global information content) must each be unique. Scattered multiple centers without a unified core receive no 10% matching bonus; at the proxy parameter stage, physical multi-center scattering causes the proportion model to fail to converge, directly setting `final_match` to zero, categorized as `proportion_model_fail`.
- Final matching score = Raw module weighted matching score + Center bonus score, must be ≥ 70% to proceed to the next layer; otherwise marks `proportion_model_fail`.

**Layer Three: 20-Item Static Physiological Structure Adaptation Check (Any single failure directly judges as Fail)**  
1. Five major system morphological adaptations ≥ 65%, single module deviation ≤ 8%;
2. Eye tension meets 1~2.5mm coverage standard (exempt for non-standard viewing angles);
3. Skin-flesh adheres to skeletal undulations;
4. Whole-face muscle tension is unified;
5. Adjacent skeletal undulation difference within legal interval: neither exceeds mutation upper limit (≤2.0), nor falls below smoothness lower limit (≥0.15, local anatomical landmark regions);
6. Skin texture intensity within bidirectional interval: $0.70 \leq \text{ratio} \leq 0.92$;
7. Facial feature system boundaries within bidirectional interval: does not invade adjacent regions (distance not less than 70% of standard), nor excessively sparse and detached (distance not more than 180% of standard);
8. Skin texture sharpness not exceeding the 0.82 upper limit, simulating the optical blur ceiling of human vision (Campbell & Robson, 1968);
9. Face possesses natural light-shadow gradation: global brightness ≤ 0.88 and shadow region area ≥ 12% (Ramachandran, 1988; Li et al., 2014);
10. Skin blood-oxygen chroma fluctuation within bidirectional interval: $0.03 \leq a^* \text{ fluctuation} \leq 0.10$ (Anderson & Parrish, 1981);
11. Regional skin texture direction variance within bidirectional interval: $0.15 \leq \text{variance} \leq 0.60$ (Pierard et al., 2000; Cula et al., 2004; Langer, 1861; Borges, 1984);
12. **Anatomical Edge Gradient Dynamic Check (added v1.2.0):** Contour edge gradient upper limit dynamically adjusted with illumination angle. When strong light is parallel to the surface normal, the gradient upper limit is greatly reduced (lines must be blurry and weakened); when the normal is perpendicular to the light source, the original upper limit is maintained. If AI forcibly draws clear texture in strong light areas where it should not exist, intercept (Ruderman & Bialek, 1994);
13. **Skeleton-Linked Shadow Depth Upper Limit Check (added v1.2.0):** Based on Lambert's cosine attenuation law, calculates the theoretical brightness upper limit for each facial region. If the upstream measured brightness exceeds 1.2 times the theoretical upper limit, it indicates impossible illumination in a skeletal occlusion zone, flagging optical logic anomaly (Debevec & Malik, 1997; Lambert, 1760);
14. **Dark Region Brightness Lower Limit Check (added v2.0.1):** Measured brightness must not be less than $L_{\max} \times 0.08$, intercepting dead black;
15. **Texture Intensity Upper Limit Check (added v2.1.0):** Intercepts sandpaper skin/over-sharpening;
16. **Hemoglobin Chroma Fluctuation Upper Limit Check (added v2.1.0):** Intercepts bruising/rash-like appearance;
17. **Texture Direction Variance Upper Limit Check (added v2.1.0):** Intercepts random chaotic texture;
18. **Feature Boundary Sparse Detachment Check (added v2.1.0):** Intercepts sparse, detached facial features;
19. **Skeletal Undulation Over-smoothness Check (added v2.1.0):** Intercepts skeletal flattening/pebble face;
20. **Texture Intensity Spatial Compliance Check (added v2.3.0):** Upgrades texture intensity constraint from a full-face scalar to a spatial vector. Baseline check: the minimum texture intensity of all sampling windows across the full face $\geq 0.70$; Spatial ordering: the texture intensity ordering of anatomical zones must conform to the living human ordering (periorbital > cheek > forehead $\approx$ mandible > nose); Regional tolerance interval (soft intercept): measured value of each zone does not exceed the expected value $\pm 3\sigma$. Violation of baseline or ordering marks `TEXTURE_SPATIAL_INVERSION`, categorized as `OVER_SMOOTH_SKIN`. See Sections 5.2.1-5.2.2 for details.

Samples with missing facial features only validate existing tissues, skipping detection of missing modules.

**Note 1:** Rules ⑧ and ⑨ are only for conventional face scenarios; extreme professional studio strong light/macro medical close-up images can be added to a business whitelist branch to avoid false interception of extreme real-life samples.

**Note 2:** Excessive skin sharpening, global exposure anomalies, absence of natural facial shadows, skin color homogenization, texture direction anomalies, lighting logic violating physics, dead-black shadows, overly strong texture, excessive blood color fluctuation, texture disorder, and spatial texture inversion are all non-biological skin/lighting distortions, uniformly reusing the `OVER_SMOOTH_SKIN` rejection label; sparse feature detachment reuses the `SYSTEM_INVASION` label; skeletal flattening reuses the `UNEVEN_CONTOUR` label. To finely differentiate defect types, extended markers can be added in the detail field without changing the top-level interception result.

**Note 3 (Theoretical Unity Statement):** The underlying logic of Rules ⑧ through ⑳ in Layer Three is unified as the Facial Optical Consistency Framework. The skin five-dimension system (⑧⑨⑩⑪) and its bidirectional extensions (⑮⑯⑰) verify: whether the skin exhibits the non-homogeneous, multi-level optical behavior characteristic of real people under multi-scale lighting response—real human skin inherently possesses synergistic features across five dimensions: texture intensity, sharpness upper limit, light-shadow gradation, hemoglobin chroma fluctuation, and texture direction variance, with each dimension within a legal interval. AI-generated images find it very difficult to simultaneously satisfy all conditions. Rule 20, building on this, upgrades texture intensity validation from a full-face scalar to a spatial vector—the texture intensity of real human skin not only has upper and lower limits for the full face, but also has a definitive ordering relationship among different anatomical regions. The three optical physics checks (⑫⑬⑭) supplement the physical upper and lower bound constraints of skeleton-lighting interaction. The eleven rules verify the same proposition from different angles: whether the lighting response of this face conforms to the optical laws of a living body. The theoretical root of Rule 11's bidirectional threshold (0.15-0.60) is detailed in Chapter Five—its essence is the joint signature of skin tension line anisotropy (bottom-layer unity) and epidermal renewal randomness (surface-layer randomness) on texture direction statistics.

### 4.4 Threshold Calibration Rules
Baseline thresholds are calibrated based on adults aged 20-40, using the mean ±2 standard deviations of real human face samples in this age group; segmented offset coefficients are configured for infants/toddlers and the elderly due to soft tissue differences. The current demonstration code is only adapted for adults; full-age-range sample pool expansion is required for deployment, supplementing offset parameters for each age segment.

**Physical logic of age offsets:**  
- **Infants/Toddlers:** Subcutaneous fat layer is thick, and bony prominences are smoothed by fat. This is equivalent to applying a spatial low-pass filter to the skeletal undulation signal—not that the bones are not developed, but the developing bone information is attenuated on the skin surface. Therefore, the skeletal undulation tolerance in the Shape Formula needs to be relaxed, and the skeletal undulation smoothness lower limit needs to be correspondingly lowered. Simultaneously, subcutaneous fat scattering is strong, increasing the weight of the subsurface scattering term in the Material-Lighting Formula; muscle tension is lower but uniformly distributed, so the whole-face tension unity of Rule 4 still applies, but the absolute tension value is lowered.
- **Elderly:** Subcutaneous fat atrophies, skin elasticity decreases. The skeletal undulation signal is enhanced, making the skeletal adherence check more sensitive. Note: the sensitivity of the skeletal adherence check should differentiate anatomical regions: bony prominent areas such as the zygomatic bone and brow ridge have enhanced signals due to fat atrophy; but areas such as the mandible and cheeks, due to skin laxity and gravity sagging, may present a false positive of "soft tissue detaching from bone"—this is not an AI physiological violation but a physical manifestation of real aging. Rule 3's skin-flesh adherence must have thresholds calibrated by region to avoid misjudging real aging as silicone face. Additionally, skin texture sharpness decreases but texture intensity does not necessarily decrease, so Rules 6 and 8 need separate handling; hemoglobin chroma fluctuation may decrease due to microcirculation degradation, requiring the Rule 10 threshold to be lowered; the skeletal undulation smoothness lower limit needs adjustment based on specific bone resorption patterns.

**Threshold Calibration Status Statement**  
The following parameters are initial estimates based on limited samples; production environments require calibration on large-scale real human samples before being used for interception decisions:
- Texture intensity upper limit 0.92
- Hemoglobin chroma fluctuation upper limit 0.10
- Texture direction variance upper limit 0.60
- Feature boundary sparse upper limit 1.80
- Skeletal undulation smoothness lower limit 0.15
- Texture intensity spatialization lookup table values (regional expected means and tolerance intervals)

The following parameters have solid physical model support and can be used directly:
- Dark region brightness lower limit 0.08 (based on subsurface scattering engineering approximation)
- Hemoglobin chroma fluctuation lower limit 0.03 (based on skin optics multi-layer model)
- Texture intensity lower limit 0.70 (based on skin surface roughness anatomical baseline)
- Texture direction variance lower limit 0.15 (based on facial dermatoglyphic zone map and skin tension line anatomical basis)
- Texture intensity spatial ordering (based on mandatory anatomical relationships of sebaceous gland density zonation, expression folding zonation, and dermal thickness differences)

Precise quantitative values for specific offset coefficients require large-scale multi-age sample calibration.

### 4.5 Standardized JSON Output Interface
The return fields include a boolean `qualified` (pass/fail), confidence (high/medium/low), a single rejection reason, and multi-dimensional risk flags (uncanny valley risk, structural conflict, center scattering, facial feature absence, low confidence, proportion anomaly, system boundary invasion, texture spatial inversion).

**Confidence Logic:** fail judgment with complete 3D data defaults to high confidence; upstream 2D data only or facial feature absence with fail is low confidence; pass with 2D data is medium confidence; pass with facial feature absence is also medium confidence; low confidence pass results require manual review.

## Five: Skin Six-Dimension Judgment System and Hierarchical Structure Theory of Texture Generation

### 5.1 Skin Six-Dimension Judgment System Overview

| Dimension | Detection Content | Interception Condition | Version Introduced |
|---|---|---|---|
| Texture Intensity | Is there texture / is texture over-strong | $\text{ratio} < 0.70$ or $\text{ratio} > 0.92$ | v1.0.4 / v2.1.0 |
| Texture Sharpness | Is texture over-sharp | $\text{sharpness} > 0.82$ | v1.0.9 |
| Light-Shadow Gradation | Is there light-dark / are dark areas dead-black | $\text{brightness} > 0.88$ or $\text{shadow} < 0.12$ or $L < L_{\max}\times 0.08$ | v1.0.9 / v2.0.1 |
| Hemoglobin Chroma | Is skin color homogeneous / excessively fluctuating | $a^*$ fluctuation $< 0.03$ or $a^*$ fluctuation $> 0.10$ | v1.1.0 / v2.1.0 |
| Texture Direction | Is skin texture direction varied / disordered | variance $< 0.15$ or variance $> 0.60$ | v1.1.0 / v2.1.0 |
| Texture Intensity Spatial | Is regional ordering correct | Spatial inversion (nasal > periorbital, etc.) | v2.3.0 |

All six rules must be passed to obtain a skin pass. AI-generated images, under current technical conditions, find it difficult to satisfy all conditions simultaneously, whereas real humans naturally satisfy all. Bidirectional constraints ensure the model cannot find safe solutions at the opposite extreme. Spatial ordering constraints further ensure the model cannot circumvent constraints through spatial redistribution within the legal numerical interval.

### 5.2 Deep Theory of Texture Direction: Bottom-Layer Unity · Surface-Layer Randomness
Rule 11 constrains texture direction variance within the bidirectional interval of 0.15-0.60. The physical inevitability of this interval stems from the hierarchical generation structure of living human skin—it is not an aesthetic preference or statistical fit, but a joint signature of two levels strictly determined by skin anatomy and biomechanics.

**Layer 1: Bottom-Layer Unity — Anisotropy of Skin Tension Lines**  
Skin is not a homogeneous isotropic material. Collagen and elastic fibers in the dermis are woven in specific directions, forming the skin's tensile anisotropy—the skin has different mechanical properties in different directions. The anatomical expression of this phenomenon is the skin tension lines (Langer, 1861; Borges, 1984). On the face, Langer's lines have extremely regular zonal orientations: transverse on the forehead, circumferential around the eyes, radial around the mouth, longitudinal on the mandible. This means that within each anatomical zone, the main orientation of collagen fibers is unified—this is the anatomical root of "bottom-layer unity". It strictly constrains the main texture direction on the skin surface within that region: the texture cannot be randomly distributed; it must follow the fiber orientation.

**Layer 2: Surface-Layer Randomness — Asynchrony of Epidermal Renewal**  
Above the unified directional framework of the bottom layer, the microstructure of the epidermal layer is driven by asynchronous cellular biological processes. The differentiation and desquamation of keratinocytes, the transfer and deposition of melanosomes, the secretion cycle of sebaceous glands—these processes are not globally coordinated but possess natural random variation at the millimeter scale (Pierard et al., 2000; Kligman, 1964). The result is that within any local sampling window in the same anatomical region, pore sizes are unequal, pigmentation is uneven, and the depth and branching morphology of skin furrows vary. This is the cellular biological root of "surface-layer randomness".

**The Inevitability of the Hierarchical Structure**  
Bottom-layer unity and surface-layer randomness are not two independent phenomena but the inevitable result of the mechanical structure of skin as a fiber-reinforced composite material (Daly, 1982). Collagen fibers are mechanically oriented during embryonic development and solidify into the principal tension line network; the epidermis, throughout life, is in a continuous, cellular-level asynchronous renewal. The former is unchangeable (except by trauma or surgery), the latter uncontrollable (except by global disease). Their superposition produces a texture signature that is precisely describable statistically: within a single anatomical zone, the main direction of texture is highly consistent (variance lower limit 0.15), but the specific morphology of the texture—depth, width, branching—is highly variable, and the main direction between zones shows natural differences (variance upper limit 0.60).

**Why Diffusion Models Cannot Do It**  
This hierarchical structure reveals the fundamental dilemma of AI skin generation. The denoising process of a diffusion model is a noise attenuation process that smoothly evolves across multiple spatial frequency scales. Living human skin requires noise to maintain high spatial correlation at the macroscopic scale (low frequency) to preserve directional unity, while simultaneously losing spatial correlation at the microscopic scale (high frequency) to achieve random variation. This means the correlation structure of noise must undergo a jump across scales—from correlated to uncorrelated. The denoising steps of a diffusion model transition smoothly across scales; they do not jump. Therefore, it can only go to two extremes: either the correlation persists down to the microscopic scale (full-face streaking, variance < 0.15), or the correlation is already lost at the macroscopic scale (local chaotic texture, variance > 0.60). Rule 11's bidirectional threshold essentially intercepts a structural defect of diffusion models in texture generation that is irreparable—not because it "doesn't draw well enough", but because "the generation mechanism itself does not allow hierarchical jumps".

Supporting literature: Langer (1861), Borges (1984), Daly (1982), Pierard et al. (2000), Kligman (1964).

#### 5.2.1 Texture Intensity Baseline: The Unbreakable 0.70 for the Full Face
The logical starting point of spatial texture intensity validation is a physical fact more fundamental than texture direction:

For a living human, in any anatomical region, as long as the epidermis is normally renewing, the texture intensity at the microscopic scale (5×5mm sampling window) cannot be below 0.70. This is not a statistical threshold, but determined by the minimum surface roughness of the stratum corneum in a state without sebum film coverage and with normal desquamation (Quan et al., 1997).

Below 0.70 means epidermal renewal is suppressed or the surface is overfilled—in a living human, this can only occur in scar tissue, large sebaceous nevi, or areas with excessive filler. If an AI-generated face shows a texture intensity below 0.70 in a normal cheek region, it means the model did not learn the physical baseline of epidermal renewal.

Check method: Take the minimum texture intensity across all preset sampling windows on the full face; if below 0.70, directly intercept without proceeding to subsequent regional checks. This step does not look at the mean, only the worst window. One window below 0.70, the entire face fails.

#### 5.2.2 Regional Expectation and Spatial Ordering of Texture Intensity
Above the 0.70 baseline, the texture intensity in different anatomical regions of a living human exhibits a definitive ordering relationship. This ordering is jointly determined by three anatomical factors:

1. **Expression Muscle Folding Accumulation (periorbital, glabellar, nasolabial fold):** Each muscle contraction forms micro-displacements at the fold sites; long-term accumulation deepens skin furrows. The texture depth in the lateral canthal area can be 1.3-1.5 times that of the cheek (Pierard et al., 2000).
2. **Negative Correlation with Sebaceous Gland Density (T-zone, nasal wings):** Lipids secreted by sebaceous glands fill epidermal furrows, reducing surface roughness. Sebum secretion rate on the nose can be more than 4 times that on the cheeks (Youn et al., 2005); high-sebum areas have texture closer to the baseline. Note: nasal wing pores are large, but pores are not texture intensity—pores are another dimension. Texture intensity looks at skin furrow depth and density; both are lower in sebum-rich areas.
3. **Dermal Thickness Difference:** The thicker the papillary dermis, the faster the epidermal turnover, the deeper the skin furrows. Cheek dermal thickness is about twice that of the eyelid (Ha et al., 2005).

The combination of these three factors produces the irreversible texture intensity ordering of the living human face:

**Periorbital (lateral canthus) > Nasolabial fold > Mid-cheek > Central forehead $\approx$ Mandible > Nasal wings**

AI-generated faces often violate this ordering—the typical defect is generating rougher texture in the T-zone (high-sebum area) than on the cheeks, or periorbital texture weaker than the nasal wings. The full-face mean cannot capture such spatial inversion.

Check logic (baseline and ordering are hard intercepts; tolerance intervals are currently soft intercepts):

```

Condition 1: Baseline Check (Hard)

assert min(texture_ratio_all_windows) >= 0.70

Condition 2: Spatial Ordering (Hard)

assert texture_ratio(periorbital) > texture_ratio(cheek) > texture_ratio(nose)

Condition 3: Regional Tolerance Interval (Soft intercept, upgraded to hard after calibration)

assert E_i - 3sigma <= texture_ratio_i <= E_i + 3sigma, for all i in ROIs

```

Any single failure $\to$ `OVER_SMOOTH_SKIN`, with detail sub-classifications marking the anomaly type.

Note: The current v2.3.0 only implements hard ordering checks for the three regions with the strongest mandatory ordering relationship (periorbital, cheek, nose). The texture intensity of the nasolabial fold and mid-cheek have partial overlap, requiring finer data calibration before inclusion in hard ordering. There is no anatomically mandatory ordering relationship between the forehead and mandible (sebaceous gland density and frontalis muscle folding effects cancel each other out), so they are linked with $\approx$ and not subject to hard constraints. The ordering relationships of the remaining regions are reserved for v2.4.0 extension.

Engineering implementation uses a lookup table method. The following values are initial estimates based on limited samples (consistent with the threshold calibration status statement in Section 4.4, requiring calibration on large-scale real human samples):

| ROI | Expected Mean $E_i$ | Lower Tolerance ($E_i-3\sigma$) | Upper Tolerance ($E_i+3\sigma$) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nose | 0.73 | 0.70 | 0.82 |
| Forehead | 0.77 | 0.71 | 0.85 |
| Cheek | 0.80 | 0.73 | 0.89 |
| Mandible | 0.78 | 0.71 | 0.86 |
| Periorbital | 0.85 | 0.77 | 0.92 |

Note: Tolerance intervals are currently set at $\pm 3\sigma$ to reduce false positive risk, to be tightened to $\pm 2\sigma$ after large-scale calibration. Spatial ordering (periorbital > cheek > nose) does not depend on precise calibration, is an anatomically mandatory relationship, and can be used directly as a hard intercept.

## Six: Facial Optical Physics Validation System (v1.2.0 / v2.0.1 / v2.1.0 / v2.2.0 / v2.3.0)

### 6.1 Core Idea: From Empirical Mapping to Physical Laws
The skin lighting checks in v1.1.0 and earlier (brightness, shadow area, sharpness upper limit) used static thresholds, unable to adapt to varying shooting lighting conditions. v1.2.0 upgrades the validation logic to a unified optical transfer equation—no longer calibrating empirical thresholds for each lighting angle, but using a single physical formula, substituting light source parameters and skeletal geometry, to compute in real-time the theoretical light-dark interval and structural visibility upper limit for each facial region. v2.0.1 supplements the lower bound constraint to eliminate dead black. v2.1.0 extends this bidirectional constraint to skin material parameters such as texture intensity, hemoglobin chroma, and texture direction. v2.2.0 supplements the penumbra gradient upper-bound constraint, extending lighting verification from "is the brightness correct" (Lambertian) to "is the transition correct" (penumbra). v2.3.0 upgrades the texture intensity bidirectional constraint from a full-face scalar to a spatial vector—not only constraining the full-face upper and lower limits, but also constraining the ordering relationship among regions.

### 6.2 Regional Theoretical Brightness Upper Limit Formula (Lambertian Check)
For any facial region, the theoretical maximum brightness under a given light source is calculated by Lambert's cosine attenuation law combined with skeletal occlusion.

Check rule: If the upstream measured brightness exceeds 1.2 times the theoretical upper limit (tolerance coefficient to absorb measurement noise from upstream feature extraction), it indicates impossible highlights in a skeletal occlusion dark area—i.e., the AI's "full revelation" lighting bug, directly intercepted.

### 6.3 Penumbra Gradient Upper Limit Check (Added v2.2.0)
The Lambertian check answers "are the dark areas dark enough". But correct brightness values do not equal correct transition methods. Light sources in the real world are never ideal geometric points—the sun has a 0.5-degree angular diameter, lightboxes have emitting area, windows have size. The spatial extent of the light source means that the shadow edge produced by skeletal occlusion must have a penumbra zone with continuous brightness transition (Rayleigh, 1896). The pixel width of this transition zone is determined by the light source angular size, the distance from the occluder to the projection surface, and the distance from the light source to the occluder. In real photographs, the transition width of facial shadow boundaries typically falls between 5-20 pixels (Hasenfratz et al., 2003). There is no shadow edge of zero width—light never gives a step; it only gives a ramp.

AI diffusion models treat all edges equally—it learns that "there should be a gradient here", but does not understand how wide that gradient should be, because it does not differentiate the physical cause of edges (texture edges can be sharp; shadow edges, constrained by penumbra, must be gradual). The result is that AI can generate a hard transition of 1-2 pixels at the skeletal shadow boundary—jumping directly from black to white without intermediate transition colors.

Check rule: For shadow boundaries produced by skeletal occlusion, the pixel width of the measured brightness transition zone must not be less than the theoretical minimum penumbra width calculated from the light source angular size and occlusion geometry. If AI compresses the transition below this lower limit, mark `penumbra_violation`, categorized as `OVER_SMOOTH_SKIN` rejection.

This rule complements Rule 12 (anatomical edge gradient dynamic check): Rule 12 limits unreasonable sharpening of texture edges under strong light; the penumbra check limits unreasonable hardening of shadow edges under any lighting. Together, they ensure all brightness transitions on the face fall within the physically permissible range.

### 6.4 Regional Brightness Lower Limit Formula (Added v2.0.1)

### 6.10 Theoretical Cornerstone: The Distortion Invariance Theorem of Lighting Rationality
This section provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for the facial optical physics validation (Sections 6.2 to 6.9). Core proposition:

Any real photograph that has not undergone local malicious tampering will always have self-consistent lighting logic. If an AI model judges such a photograph to have "unreasonable lighting", that judgment is invariably a false positive error of the model.

#### 6.10.1 Physical Light Field and Lighting Consistency
Let a real 3D scene contain the following physical entities:
- Surface geometry $\mathcal{S} \subset \mathbb{R}^3$, normal vector field $\mathbf{n}(p), \ p \in \mathcal{S}$;
- Principal light source direction $\mathbf{l} \in \mathbb{S}^2$ (multiple light source superposition allowed, but the superposition law globally obeys the same physical model);
- Occlusion relationships are deterministically given by the geometric occlusion of $\mathcal{S}$ with respect to $\mathbf{l}$.

**Definition (Physical Lighting Consistency).** The physical lighting of this scene is called consistent if and only if:
1. **Light Source Constraint:** There exists a unique principal light source direction $\mathbf{l}$ (or fixed superposition weights for multiple sources) that is the same for all points in the scene.
2. **Luminance Constraint:** The diffuse reflected radiance of any non-occluded point $p \in \mathcal{S}$ follows Lambert's cosine law (Lambert, 1760):
   $$L(p) = \rho(p) \cdot (\mathbf{n}(p) \cdot \mathbf{l})$$
   where $\rho(p)$ is the surface albedo. If $p$ is in shadow, then $L(p) = 0$ (ignoring ambient light).
3. **Geometric Constraint:** Shadow boundaries are deterministic projections of occlusion geometry; highlight positions are geometric neighborhoods where the surface normal direction reflects the light source exactly towards the camera.

#### 6.10.2 Imaging Model and the Mathematical Essence of Distortion
**Ideal Imaging.** The scene is projected via the camera optical system onto the sensor plane, producing an ideal (undistorted) image:
$$I(x, y), \quad (x, y) \in \Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2$$
$I(x,y)$ is the sensor irradiance after lens transmission attenuation of the scene radiance, a faithful record of the physical light field. $I$ inherits all physical lighting consistency constraints.

**Lens Distortion.** The physical cause of distortion is that the lens magnifies light rays at different incident angles differently. Denote the distortion mapping as:
$$\Phi: \Omega \to \Omega', \quad (x', y') = \Phi(x, y)$$
**Core property: $\Phi$ is a diffeomorphism.** That is:
- Continuous: pixels do not disappear or tear out of nowhere;
- Differentiable: local transformation is smooth, does not introduce new edges;
- Bijective: each object point maps to a unique image point, and the mapping is invertible;
- Jacobian determinant nowhere zero: $\det J_\Phi \neq 0$, neighborhood relationships are fully preserved.

The distorted image $J$ is:
$$J(x', y') = I\left(\Phi^{-1}(x', y')\right)$$
Crucial fact: $\Phi$ does not create, modify, or delete any brightness value. Each pixel value of $J$ is an exact copy of some pixel value in $I$, only its spatial position has been rearranged. All information of the physical light field—including light source direction, occlusion relationships, surface normals—is preserved intact in $J$.

#### 6.10.3 Core Theorem and Its Proof
**Theorem (Distortion Invariance of Lighting Rationality).** Let an ideal image $I$ satisfy all physical lighting consistency constraints defined in Section 6.10.1. For any diffeomorphic planar mapping $\Phi$, the distorted image $J = I \circ \Phi^{-1}$ has exactly the same underlying physical light field as $I$; therefore, its lighting logic is necessarily physically self-consistent.

*Proof.* The pixel value of $J$ at coordinate $(x', y')$ equals the pixel value of $I$ at $\Phi^{-1}(x', y')$. The pixel value of $I$ at that point records the radiance of some 3D point $p \in \mathcal{S}$ in the scene. The physical illumination received by that point—light source direction $\mathbf{l}$, surface normal $\mathbf{n}(p)$, occlusion state—is a function of $p$, independent of the position where $p$ projects onto the sensor. $\Phi$ merely changes the projection position, not $p$ itself, and therefore does not change the illumination properties of $p$. This reasoning holds for all pixels in $J$. Hence, all pixels of $J$ share the same physical light field, and its lighting logic is completely consistent with $I$.

**(a) Local Structure Preservation.** Since $\Phi$ is a diffeomorphism, its Jacobian determinant is everywhere non-zero. Therefore: adjacent pixels in $I$ remain adjacent in $J$—neighborhood relationships are fully preserved, no tearing or folding; the brightness gradient transforms via the chain rule $\nabla J = \nabla I \cdot J_{\Phi^{-1}}$, which is a linear transformation; gradient direction is rotated and scaled, but the topology of the gradient field remains unchanged. Shadow transitions remain continuous.

**(b) Light Source Direction Consistency Preservation.** The physical light source direction $\mathbf{l}$ is a scene 3D property, independent of pixel coordinates. Every pixel of $J$ corresponds to the same 3D point $p \in \mathcal{S}$ as $I$, sharing the same $\mathbf{l}$, the same $\mathbf{n}(p)$, and the same occlusion state. Therefore, there exists a single globally consistent physical lighting explanation that simultaneously explains the brightness values of all pixels in $J$.

**(c) Shadow and Highlight Boundary Continuity.** Suppose $I$ contains a shadow boundary curve $\Gamma$, produced by the occlusion of the scene geometry against $\mathbf{l}$. $\Gamma$ in $I$ is a continuous curve, and the brightness on both sides satisfies a definite light-dark relationship. In $J$, this boundary becomes $\Phi(\Gamma)$. Since $\Phi$ is continuous, $\Phi(\Gamma)$ remains a continuous curve. The brightness relationship on both sides of the boundary is fully preserved. The same holds for highlights. $\blacksquare$

#### 6.10.4 Corollary: The Mathematical Flaw of Malicious Tampering
Consider local "malicious Photoshopping": in a region $R \subset \Omega'$, the pixels of $J$ are replaced with corresponding content from a foreign image $I_{\text{foreign}}$. If $I_{\text{foreign}}$ was not generated from the same physical light field through the same camera model (including the same $\Phi$), then within $R$:
$$J_{\text{fake}}(x', y') = I_{\text{foreign}}(x', y'), \quad (x', y') \in R$$
In this case, the lighting, geometry, and camera parameters within $R$ are inconsistent with those outside $R$. At the boundary of $R$, at least one of the following flaws must appear: luminance discontinuity, gradient discontinuity, inconsistent physical light source direction, or shadow/highlight logic mismatch.

Conversely: If all pixels of an image are generated by the same physical light field through the same optical system (i.e., no local foreign replacement has occurred), then distortion—no matter how severe—will never destroy its lighting consistency.

#### 6.10.5 Engineering Significance: Strict Constraints on AI Judgment Capability
**Corollary 1 (Inevitability of AI False Positive Judgments).** If all pixels of a certain image are generated by the same physical light field through a single optical system (including lens distortion), and it has not undergone local malicious tampering, then its lighting logic is necessarily self-consistent. Any AI model that judges such an image as having "inconsistent lighting" or "abnormal lighting logic" is making a judgment that is necessarily a false positive—the error lies not in the image, but in the model's own ignorance of physical laws.

**Corollary 2 (Mathematical Uniqueness of White-Box Rule Validation).** The judgment logic of pure neural networks (large language models, diffusion models, etc.) is black-box probability matching, unable to provide the deterministic guarantee that the theorem relies on. Only a white-box rule engine—where every rule is an explicit encoding of a physical law—can provide a mathematically irrefutable justification for a "lighting inconsistency" judgment.

**Corollary 3 (Support for the Validation Methods in Sections 6.2-6.7).** The skeleton-linked shadow depth check, structural visibility check, and penumbra gradient upper limit check proposed in this chapter have their underlying logic based on the brightness upper limit calculation from Lambert's law and the penumbra constraint from light source angular size. The theorem provides strict boundary conditions for these calculations: if the measured brightness exceeds the theoretical upper limit, or the shadow transition is narrower than the minimum penumbra width, there is no possible defense like "maybe it's caused by lens distortion"—distortion has been proven incapable of causing such exceedances.
